The Invention Of The Internet

The Internet is considered to be one of the greatest inventions of the twenty-first century. The greatness of it is on the same plane as the printing press or even the wheel because of how impactful it is in shaping our civilization.

How Did the Internet Begin

Dating back to the 1950s, a psychologist and computer scientist named J.C.R. Licklider envisioned that computers would allow us to communicate better and allow all computers to connect together to help share information better. His vision wasn't realized until 1960s when Leonard Kleinrock began applying his queuing theory to computers that led to the base of data transmission. Queuing theory is similar in concept to that of the USPS mailing process where the mail queue in line until they are released into their designated locations. He implemented two techniques, demand access and distributed control, in order to speed up the network to aid the data transmission process.

Soviet Fear

The military in the early 60s, America feared a Soviet nuclear attack would level American cities that the telephone system would be so damaged that the military would be unable to launch a counter-attack. To assure retaliation, America needed an indestructible communications network. Paul Baran was hired to see if one could be built. He and his team began by playing with fishnet type networks so that if you chop it up there's still a path through the network.

ARPA - Advanced Research Project

In the early 1960s surfing was the search for the perfect wave mail was only delivered by the post office online meant waiting behind someone at the movie theater and a server was a waitress at your favorite drive-in. Frustrations of a single ARPA - Advanced Research Projects - scientist Bob Taylor, due to no two computers could talk to each other, Taylor had to move from terminal to terminal to work on a different computer. He knew a single terminal could suffice if all the computers were in a common Network. Taylor wanted to build a network because he thought it was crazy that he had to have three different terminals to talk to three different machines instead of one per mole. Taylor asked his boss for money to build a computer network arguing that it could save ARPA time money and resources, and he was allocated 1 million dollars to turn the theories of a computer network into a reality. This was enough to start a chain reaction that changed the world of course at the time nobody knew it we all knew that we were dealing with very powerful technology.
